The Challenge

The employee app shipped. Who runs the building?

FlexiSpace gave employees a beautiful way to book. But the people managing the workplace were still flying blind — across tools that never talked to each other.

01

“Is the office even full?”

No live read on occupancy. Planning ran on guesswork, badge exports and spreadsheets that were stale by lunchtime.

02

“Why are desks empty but booked?”

No-shows quietly locked up inventory all day. There was no way to see the waste, let alone reclaim it automatically.

03

“Which tool do I open?”

Floor edits, room config, parking and access lived in four separate apps — plus a thread of emails for every change.

04

“Are we paying for space we don’t use?”

No utilization or cost-per-seat view. Right-sizing the portfolio was a finance exercise done once a year, in the dark.

The Opportunity

One console to run the workplace.

Every question above is really one need: see the whole building live, then act on it — without leaving the screen.

01

A live command center

Occupancy, rooms, parking and alerts on one always-current screen — no refresh, no export.

02

Manage the floor in 3D

Edit layouts, flag maintenance and reassign desks directly on the real, interactive plan.

03

Policy & access in one place

Anchor days, auto-release, roles and SSO become simple toggles — not tickets.

04

Decisions backed by data

Utilization, attendance and cost per seat, so space planning is evidence, not instinct.

Design Principles

Five rules for an operator’s tool.

P01

Legible at a Glance

The whole building reads in one scan — hierarchy and headline numbers before the detail.

P02

One Status Language

The same color and dot system reads identically on the map, the lists and the sheets.

P03

Live, Not Refreshed

Numbers move on their own. “Updated 1m ago” and a quiet pulse keep the data honest.

P04

Insight → Action

Every chart carries its next step — reassign, release, manage — never a dead end.

P05

Adaptive Surface

Light or dark, airy or compact, sidebar or top-nav — the console fits the operator and the screen.

Reflections

What I took away.

What I learned

A dashboard is a decision tool.

Numbers alone don’t help an operator. Pairing every chart with its next action — reassign, release, manage — was what made the console feel useful, not just informative.

A trade-off

Calm over cramming.

Operators ask for density, but a wall of numbers hides the signal. I shipped an airy default with a compact mode, rather than packing everything in by force.

What I’d improve

Define the live data model first.

“Live, not refreshed” touched every surface. Next time I’d agree the real-time data contracts with engineering in week one, not retrofit them.

How it changed me

Designing for trust, not delight.

The employee app earns smiles; the console earns confidence. Reusing one status language across both taught me to design the system, not the screen.
