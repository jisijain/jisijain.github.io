01 · Problem

UPSC aspirants drown in content — and still feel unprepared

The UPSC Civil Services Examination is India's most competitive exam. Aspirants spend 12–18 months preparing, yet most fail not from lack of effort — but from a broken study system.

😩

Content Fragmentation

Aspirants juggle 5+ apps daily: YouTube for concepts, Telegram PDFs for current affairs, separate apps for MCQs, physical notes for revision. Nothing talks to anything else.

📉

No Progress Visibility

You can read for 8 hours and have no idea if you're ready for the exam. No system tells you what you know, what you don't, or what to study next.

🔁

Passive Learning Loop

Aspirants re-read the same chapters hoping it'll stick. There's no spaced repetition, no active recall, no feedback mechanism — just reading as a proxy for learning.

🕐

Consistency Collapse

Motivation is volatile. Aspirants study intensely for 2 weeks, then disappear for 10 days. No system catches them when they drop, or guides them back in.

"I have five browsers open, three Telegram groups, two physical books, and I still don't know if I'll be ready. I'm studying all the time but I feel like I'm going nowhere." — UPSC aspirant, 2nd attempt, interviewed during research

02 · Research

Understanding how aspirants actually study

8 in-depth interviews (45–60 min each), a screener survey (34 respondents), and competitive analysis across 6 existing UPSC apps.

Research Methods

Week 1 · Days 1–3
Screener Survey
34-respondent survey to map study habits, app usage patterns, daily time investment, and current pain points. Recruited via UPSC subreddit and Telegram groups.
Week 1 · Days 4–7
In-depth Interviews
8 semi-structured interviews (45–60 min each) across 2 segments: working professionals (4) and fresh graduates/full-time aspirants (4). Screened for 1st and 2nd attempt.
Week 2 · Days 1–3
Contextual Inquiry
Shadowed 3 aspirants through a 2-hour study session. Observed real tool-switching behaviour, note-taking systems, and how they self-assess comprehension.
Week 2 · Days 4–5
Competitor Analysis
Audited 6 apps: Unacademy, BYJU's, Testbook, StudyIQ, Vision IAS app, and Drishti IAS. Evaluated against 12 criteria including content integration, revision tools, and personalisation.

Key Interview Insights

💡
The "study guilt" problem Aspirants feel guilty for not studying, even on study days — because they can't measure if what they did was enough.
🧩
PYQs are the north star Every aspirant mentioned PYQs as the #1 way to know if they're on track. None had a structured way to use them diagnostically.
⚡
Short sessions are preferred Working professionals preferred 15–20 min focused sessions over 2-hour blocks. No app was designed around this reality.
🔄
Revision is the bottleneck All aspirants said they read good content but couldn't retain it. Revision felt impossible to schedule manually.

Why 4 tabs, in this order?

Every session begins here. The app answers "what should I study?" before the user even asks. Streak + targets create accountability without pressure.

Home Screen

Your daily command centre

Home answers the #1 cognitive burden of every UPSC aspirant: "What should I study today?" The screen removes that decision entirely, replacing it with a clear, personalised directive.

Streak + target combo — Streaks build intrinsic motivation; daily targets set a concrete, achievable daily milestone. Together they drive consistency without burnout.
Subject progress bars — Visual representation of mastery across each UPSC subject. Orange = low, makes weak areas immediately obvious without any navigation.
AI-recommended next — The card at the bottom adapts daily based on what you studied, what you got wrong, and what's due for spaced repetition.
One-tap entry — Every element on Home is a direct entry point into the relevant surface — tap the Polity bar to jump straight to weak Polity cards in Scroll.
Scroll

Micro-learning built for micro-sessions

Research showed that aspirants have 15–25 minute pockets throughout their day — commutes, lunch breaks, waiting rooms. Scroll is designed for exactly this context.

Swipeable card format — Modelled on the cognitive familiarity of social media scroll, but redirected toward learning. Zero learning curve.
Got it / Review later — Binary feedback after every card. "Got it" marks the topic as understood; "Review later" queues it for spaced repetition. This data feeds the AI layer.
Rich content types — Cards adapt to content: text summaries, maps as images, timelines as interactive flows, current affairs as bite-sized news.
Session progress counter — "Card 3 of 24" tells you how deep into a topic you are, and how long you'll need. Respects the user's time.

Learn

Deep dive when depth is needed

While Scroll handles quick sessions, Learn handles systematic subject study. Full syllabus navigation organised as subjects → units → topics → articles → cards — mirroring how aspirants mentally model the exam.

Subject-level progress — Each subject card shows % completion at a glance. The aspirant always knows how far they are without opening anything.
Syllabus breadcrumbs — Always shows the path: Subject → Unit → Topic. Prevents the "lost in content" feeling that every competitor app creates.
Read and card both available — Each article has a long-form read mode and a card mode. Users pick depth based on their time and familiarity with the topic.
7 core subjects — History, Polity, Geography, Economy, Science & Tech, Environment, Current Affairs — mapped precisely to the UPSC syllabus.
Strategy

The feedback loop that closes the learning cycle

Strategy is where learning becomes exam-readiness. PYQs aren't just practice — they're diagnostic tools. The tab shows which weak areas are most likely to cost marks, in descending urgency.

AI-ranked weak areas — Not just "what you got wrong" but "what's likely to appear, that you're weak in" — cross-referencing your performance with historical PYQ frequency.
PYQ by topic — Practice previous year questions filtered exactly to the topic you're weak in. Direct link from diagnosis to cure.
Review queue — Cards flagged as "review later" in Scroll surface here, organised by urgency using spaced repetition intervals.
Practice paths — Structured practice sequences (mock test mode, subject focus, previous year full papers) for aspirants closer to the exam.

07 · Design Decisions

The reasoning behind the choices

Why orange as primary accent?
Orange signals energy and urgency without being aggressive. In an exam context, it reads as "action" — study now, complete this — without triggering the anxiety that red would. It also contrasts strongly on dark backgrounds at small text sizes.
Why a dark theme?
50%+ of aspirants study at night or in low-light (commutes, early mornings). A dark theme reduces eye strain over long sessions and signals "focus mode" — many aspirants explicitly requested this in interviews.
Why swipe vs. tap for Scroll?
Swipe enables one-hand operation (critical for commuters). It also creates a physical metaphor for "moving through" content — each swipe is a micro-achievement, which maintains momentum and engagement better than list-tap navigation.
Why 4 tabs and not more?
Hick's Law: more options = more decision time. Users in focused study mode should not be navigating a complex menu. The 4 tabs map to the 4 stages of the mastery loop — learn, recall, deep-dive, diagnose — and nothing else.
Why show PYQ frequency in weak areas?
Aspirants don't just want to know what they're weak in — they want to know what weakness costs them most. Showing "Asked 8× in 5 years" transforms a weak area from an abstract gap into an urgent, exam-relevant fix.
Why binary feedback (Got it / Review) instead of stars?
Stars add cognitive overhead mid-session. A 1-5 star rating mid-commute on a moving train is unreliable data. Binary responses are cognitively instant, yield cleaner signal, and are easier to act on in the spaced repetition algorithm.
Why show progress as a % bar instead of a number?
A number like "42/100 topics" is demotivating early on. A 42% filled bar looks like meaningful progress. Perception of progress drives continuation — which is the core metric for UPSC prep success.
Why is the AI layer invisible?
Aspirants trust their own judgment. Showing "AI says study this" creates resistance. Instead the AI surfaces its suggestions through natural UI elements — a recommended card, a reordered list — without labelling them as AI-driven.

After a 4-week design sprint, GyanGram launched a closed beta on Google Play. I continue to manage the beta feedback loop alongside the founding team, running iterative testing cycles to optimise navigation and content flow pre-launch.

What worked

✅
Streak drove daily habit47% of active users maintained a 7+ day streak in week 1. Riya's persona validated: the streak + target combo replaced decision fatigue with routine.
✅
Scroll dominated session starts71% of sessions started in Scroll, not Home. Aspirants found it the lowest-friction way to "get going" — exactly the intent.
✅
Strategy created "aha" momentsThe most positive feedback centred on Strategy's weak area view — users said it was "the first time I understood what I actually need to fix."

What I'd iterate next

🔄
Learn navigation needs depthUsers going deep into Learn (topics → articles) reported losing their place. Adding a persistent breadcrumb trail and a floating "back to subject" button is the highest-priority fix.
🔄
Current affairs card densityDaily current affairs cards were too long for commute use. Exploring a 3-bullet-point format with "expand" option to match Riya's 15-min session constraint.
🔄
Onboarding personalisation gapFirst-time users weren't shown how their onboarding choices shaped the Home recommendations. A brief "this is personalised for you" moment at first launch would dramatically improve perceived value.
